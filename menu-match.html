About

Break the Loop is a research project based at I2ADS — Institute of Research in Art, Design and Society, Faculty of Fine Arts, University of Porto — funded by FCT under project «2023.14448.PEX».

The project begins from a critical position towards the dominant paradigm of Human-Centred Design: by systematically prioritising frictionlessness and the removal of any obstacle to interaction, HCD has contributed to digital interfaces that exploit user behaviour through mechanisms such as infinite scroll, variable reward systems and intermittent notifications — practices that induce repetitive behavioural loops and reduce the user's capacity for reflection and critical thought.

The concept of flow (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990) describes a state of total immersion in an activity. Originally studied in contexts such as sport, music and creative work, flow was progressively adopted by interaction design as a quality criterion. This association carries an ethical tension rarely made explicit: flow as artificially prolonged by digital platforms is qualitatively distinct from the intrinsically motivated flow Csikszentmihalyi describes. The former induces not meaningful immersion but behavioural dependency.

The term dark patterns was coined by Harry Brignull in 2010 to describe interface design strategies that manipulate or deceive users into decisions they would not make if fully informed. Research shows that many designers reproduce dark patterns without full awareness of doing so — reinforcing the need for tools that make visible what tends to remain implicit in the design process.

Break the Loop proposes to research and systematise alternative strategies, drawn from game design and the notion of intentional friction, capable of restoring to the user autonomy and the capacity for conscious decision-making in their digital interactions. Friction, here, is not a failure of design — it is a deliberate act of care.

The card deck emerges in this context as a pedagogical and investigative artefact: a tangible tool that allows designers, students and practitioners to explore, argue and apply friction patterns in interface design.

The project is part of a broader reflection on the responsibility of designers in the construction of digital environments. To design is always a political act. The question is not whether our interfaces shape behaviour — they do — but whether we are willing to be accountable for how they do so.

Rules

The game is played in three modes. Each one proposes a different relationship between players, cards and the act of designing. There is no single correct way to play — only different degrees of complexity and intention.

Before each session, shuffle the Friction Patterns Deck and place it face down at the centre of the table. The Platform and Ethical Challenge decks are kept separate. The Flow cards and Connectors form the building material of the system.

Mode 1 — Archive. Individual or group exploration. No competitive structure, no problem-solving objectives. Pick a card from the Friction Patterns Deck, point the camera at it, and unlock the digital archive entry.

Archive mode has no winner. It ends when the group decides it ends. It is a vocabulary-building exercise, a slow reading of the designed world around us.

Mode 2 — Challenge. One player draws a Platform card and an Ethical Challenge card. Each other player receives three Pattern cards and secretly chooses the one they believe responds most appropriately. Cards are revealed simultaneously. Each player has one minute to defend their proposal.

Challenge mode rewards argumentation, not correctness. There is rarely a single right answer.

Mode 3 — System. One player takes the role of Designer; another becomes the Friction Agent. The Designer builds a logical user journey using Flow and Connector cards. The Friction Agent intervenes: placing Pattern cards at connection points, introducing intentional obstacles.

The result is a physical diagram — a visible record of the negotiation between usability and ethical intervention. It is never finished.
