About

Break the Loop is a research project based at I2ADS — Institute of Research in Art, Design and Society, Faculty of Fine Arts, University of Porto — funded by FCT under project «2023.14448.PEX».

The project begins from a critical position towards the dominant paradigm of Human-Centred Design: by systematically prioritising frictionlessness and the removal of any obstacle to interaction, HCD has contributed to digital interfaces that exploit user behaviour through mechanisms such as infinite scroll, variable reward systems and intermittent notifications — practices that induce repetitive behavioural loops and reduce the user's capacity for reflection and critical thought.

The concept of flow (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990) describes a state of total immersion in an activity. Originally studied in contexts such as sport, music and creative work, flow was progressively adopted by interaction design as a quality criterion. This association carries an ethical tension rarely made explicit: flow as artificially prolonged by digital platforms is qualitatively distinct from the intrinsically motivated flow Csikszentmihalyi describes. The former induces not meaningful immersion but behavioural dependency.

The term dark patterns was coined by Harry Brignull in 2010 to describe interface design strategies that manipulate or deceive users into decisions they would not make if fully informed. Research shows that many designers reproduce dark patterns without full awareness of doing so — reinforcing the need for tools that make visible what tends to remain implicit in the design process.

Break the Loop proposes to research and systematise alternative strategies, drawn from game design and the notion of intentional friction, capable of restoring to the user autonomy and the capacity for conscious decision-making in their digital interactions. Friction, here, is not a failure of design — it is a deliberate act of care.

The card deck emerges in this context as a pedagogical and investigative artefact: a tangible tool that allows designers, students and practitioners to explore, argue and apply friction patterns in interface design.

The project is part of a broader reflection on the responsibility of designers in the construction of digital environments. To design is always a political act. The question is not whether our interfaces shape behaviour — they do — but whether we are willing to be accountable for how they do so.

Rules

The Decks

Break the Loop is played with five types of cards. Each deck has a distinct function — together, they form the system.

Friction Patterns Deck — The core of the game. Each card corresponds to a pattern from the Friction Design Archive: a named, documented design strategy that introduces intentional friction into a digital interface.

Platform Deck — Each card places the game in a concrete digital context: a social media feed, a streaming service, an e-commerce checkout, and others. Platform cards anchor abstract design decisions to recognisable ecosystems.

Challenge Deck — Fifteen open questions that frame a design problem in ethical terms. These cards do not have correct answers. They are prompts for argument — each one requires players to take a position on user autonomy, attention, consent, or manipulation.

Flow Deck — Cards representing individual screen types or elements of a wireframe: onboarding screens, home feeds, settings pages, notifications, and more. Flow cards are the building material of user journeys and a diagnostic tool in Mode 3 — use them to map a real product and pinpoint exactly where an ethical tension lives before drawing a Challenge card.

Connectors — Small cards that establish the relationships between screens. Connectors define how a user moves through the journey built with Flow cards.

How to Play

Break the Loop is played in three modes. Each proposes a different relationship between players, cards, and the act of designing. There is no single correct way to play — only different degrees of complexity and intention.

Before each session, shuffle the Friction Patterns Deck and place it face down at the centre of the table. The Platform, Flow cards & Connectors and Ethical Challenge decks are kept separate.

Mode 1 — Archive

Individual or group · No time limit · No competitive structure

Pick a card from the Friction Patterns Deck. Read it. Then point your camera at it using the "Scan" function on the website to unlock its full entry in the digital archive — definition, taxonomy, and real-world examples.

There are no goals here and no wrong moves. The purpose of this mode is familiarisation: building a shared vocabulary before playing the other modes. Archive mode ends when you decide it ends. It works well at the start of a session, or as a standalone exercise when time is limited.

Suggestion: after scanning a card, try to name a digital product you use regularly that applies, or should apply, that pattern.

Mode 2 — Challenge

Argumentation-based

Challenge mode is about defending positions, not finding correct answers. A Platform card sets the context. An Ethical Challenge card frames the problem. Pattern cards are the proposed solutions. What matters is the quality of the reasoning, not consensus.

The following variants share this structure but shift who decides, who judges, and what counts as a good argument.

Variant A — Blind Draw

One player draws a Platform card and reads it aloud. A second player draws an Ethical Challenge card and reads it aloud. Each other player is dealt three Pattern cards and selects the one they believe responds most appropriately to the challenge. All cards are revealed simultaneously. Each player has one minute to defend their choice. After all defences, the group votes on the most convincing argument. The player with the most votes draws the next Platform card and the role rotates.

Variant B — Open Hand

One player draws a Platform card and an Ethical Challenge card and reads both aloud. The full Friction Patterns Deck is placed face up at the centre of the table. Players have two minutes to browse and each selects one pattern card they would apply. Cards are revealed simultaneously and each player defends their choice. This variant rewards familiarity with the archive and works best after players have completed Archive mode.

Variant C — Devil's Advocate

One player draws a Platform card and an Ethical Challenge card. One player is randomly assigned the role of Devil's Advocate — their task is to argue against the use of any friction pattern, defending the platform's perspective. All other players select a Pattern card and defend it. The group then votes: did the Devil's Advocate make a convincing case?

Variant D — Swap

One player draws one card from each deck: a Platform card, a Pattern card, and an Ethical Challenge card. They read all three aloud. They then choose any two cards to keep and discard the third — replacing it with a free choice of their own invention that they feel is more relevant, more provocative, or more honest. They present their final combination to the group and defend why they made the swap. The swap itself is part of the argument.

In all variants: challenge mode rewards argumentation and theorising, not correctness. There is rarely a single right answer.

Mode 3 — System

Cooperative or Versus

Mode 3 is a simulation. Players use Flow cards and Connectors to build a user journey — a sequence of screens that represents a real or hypothetical digital product. Friction Pattern cards are then introduced into that journey as deliberate interventions. The goal is not to win but to produce a system that can be discussed, questioned, and defended.

Diagnostic Mode One player — or the whole group — brings a real project or product they are currently working on. Instead of building a hypothetical journey, players use Flow cards to map the existing interface as accurately as possible, laying out the screens and connecting them with Connectors. Once the journey is on the table, the group draws an Ethical Challenge card and uses it as a lens: where in this flow does the tension the card describes actually live? Pattern cards are then drawn and placed not as hypothetical interventions but as proposed fixes to a real problem. A canvas is available to document both the diagnosis and the proposed intervention.

This mode works best in academic contexts where participants have a project in development, or in professional settings where a team wants to analyse an existing product.

Cooperative Mode Players collectively choose a Platform card to define the context. Together, they use Flow cards and Connectors to build a user journey, laying cards sequentially on the table to represent the screens a user moves through. Once the journey is complete, the group draws Pattern cards and must reach consensus on where — and whether — to introduce each one into the flow. A canvas is available to document the rationale for each decision.

Versus Mode One player takes the role of Designer. Another becomes the Friction Agent. A Platform card is drawn to set the context. The Designer builds a user journey using Flow cards and Connectors, aiming for a coherent and functional flow. Once the journey is laid out, the Friction Agent intervenes: drawing Pattern cards and placing them at connection points in the flow, introducing intentional obstacles. The Designer must then respond — accepting the intervention, relocating it, or arguing against it. A third player may take the role of Moderator, keeping time and framing the debate with an Ethical Challenge card drawn at the start.
